01 Percentages
5 questions ▶
📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: Percent = per hundred. Always express "X% of Y" as (X/100) × Y. The moment you see "percentage increase/decrease," use the formula directly — don't convert to decimals first.

% Change = [(New − Old) / Old] × 100
If price increases by a% then decreases by b% → Net = a + b + (ab/100) [negative = loss]
X is what % of Y → (X/Y) × 100
Successive % change: (1 + a/100)(1 + b/100) − 1 (multiply factors)
Approach for compound % questions:
①Identify base value (original price, salary, population)
②Apply multipliers: +20% → ×1.2, −15% → ×0.85
③Multiply all multipliers together, then compare to base
Speed trick: For "P% of Q = Q% of P" — use when values are reversed. If asked "40% of 75" compute "75% of 40 = 30" — same answer, easier math.
Q1 A salary is first increased by 20%, then decreased by 20%. The net change in salary is?
Easy
Solution
Net % change = a + b + ab/100 = 20 + (−20) + (20×−20)/100 = 0 − 4 = −4%
Or using multipliers: 1.2 × 0.8 = 0.96 → 4% net decrease.
Trap: Students assume it cancels to 0. It doesn't — multiplying is not the same as adding.
Answer 4% decrease
🔑 +a% then −a% never cancels. Always results in −(a²/100)% loss.
Q2 In an election, candidate A gets 55% of votes. If total votes = 80,000 and 5,000 votes were invalid, by how many valid votes does A win over B?
Medium
Solution
Valid votes = 80,000 − 5,000 = 75,000
A gets 55% and B gets 45%, so the vote difference is 10%.
10% of total valid votes is the answer: 10% of 75,000 = 7,500
Answer 7,500 votes
🔑 Always subtract invalid votes BEFORE applying percentage. Companies deliberately give total votes to trap you.
Q3 A's income is 25% more than B's. By what percent is B's income less than A's?
Medium
Solution
25% = 1/4.
Using the unit fraction shortcut: If A is 1/x more than B, then B is 1/(x+1) less than A.
Here x = 4, so B is 1/(4+1) = 1/5 less than A.
1/5 = 20%
Answer 20%
🔑 Unit Fraction Rule: 1/x more ↔ 1/(x+1) less. E.g., 1/4 more (25%) = 1/5 less (20%).
Q4 Price of a commodity falls by 20%. By what percent must consumption increase so that expenditure remains the same?
Medium
Solution
20% = 1/5.
Using the unit fraction shortcut: If Price falls by 1/x, Consumption must rise by 1/(x−1) to keep expenditure constant.
Here x = 5, so Consumption rises by 1/(5−1) = 1/4.
1/4 = 25%
Answer 25%
🔑 Unit Fraction Rule: 1/x less ↔ 1/(x−1) more. E.g., 1/5 less (20%) = 1/4 more (25%).
Q5 Population of a town increases at 10% per year. After 3 years it is 13,310. What was the population 3 years ago?
Hard
Solution
P × (1.1)³ = 13,310
(1.1)³ = 1.331
P = 13,310 / 1.331 = 10,000
Answer 10,000
🔑 Memorize: 1.1² = 1.21, 1.1³ = 1.331, 1.1⁴ = 1.4641. Saves 30 seconds per question.
02 Time & Work
7 questions▶
📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: Convert everything to "work done per day" (efficiency). Total work = 1 unit (or LCM of days for cleaner numbers).

If A takes 'a' days → A's 1-day work = 1/a
A+B together = 1/a + 1/b → Time = ab/(a+b)
LCM Method: Assume total work = LCM(a,b) units. A's rate = LCM/a per day.
Pipes: Inlet adds work, outlet subtracts work.
Approach:
①Take LCM of all given days as total work
②Find per-day efficiency of each person
③Add efficiencies for combined work, divide total work by combined efficiency
Trick for alternating days: Find work in 2-day cycle, then multiply. Handle remainder carefully.
Q1 A can do a job in 12 days, B in 18 days. They start together but A leaves 3 days before completion. In how many days is the job done?
Hard
Solution
LCM(12,18) = 36. A's rate = 3/day, B's rate = 2/day.
Last 3 days: Only B works → 3 × 2 = 6 units done.
Remaining = 36 − 6 = 30 units done by A+B together → 30/5 = 6 days.
Total = 6 + 3 = 9 days
Answer 9 days
🔑 Work backward: compute what the remaining person does in their solo period first, then find time for combined phase.
Q2 A is twice as efficient as B. Together they finish in 12 days. How many days does A alone take?
Easy
Solution
A = 2B in efficiency. Let B = 1 unit/day → A = 2 units/day.
Together: 3 units/day → 1/3 of work per day.
Together take 12 days → Total work = 12 × 3 = 36 units.
A alone = 36/2 = 18 days
Answer 18 days
🔑 "Twice as efficient" = takes half the time, does double the work per day.
Q3 Pipe A fills a tank in 6 hrs, pipe B fills in 4 hrs, pipe C empties in 8 hrs. All three open together — in how many hours is the tank full?
Medium
Solution
LCM(6,4,8) = 24. A fills 4/hr, B fills 6/hr, C empties 3/hr.
Net per hour = 4 + 6 − 3 = 7 units/hr.
Time = 24/7 = 3 3/7 hours
Answer 24/7 hrs (≈ 3.43 hrs)
🔑 Outlet/emptying pipes subtract from the combined rate. Check if answer is even possible — if outlet rate ≥ inlet rate, tank never fills.
Q4 20 men can complete a job in 16 days. After 4 days, 4 men leave. In how many more days will the remaining men complete the job?
Medium
Solution
Total work = 20 × 16 = 320 man-days.
Done in 4 days = 20 × 4 = 80 man-days.
Remaining = 320 − 80 = 240 man-days.
Remaining men = 16. Days = 240/16 = 15 days
Answer 15 more days
🔑 Men × Days = Total work (man-days). Always compute remaining work, then divide by new workforce.
Q5 A and B alternate daily starting with A. A takes 10 days alone, B takes 15 days. When will the work finish?
Hard
Solution
LCM = 30. A = 3/day, B = 2/day. In 2 days (A+B cycle) = 5 units.
30/5 = 6 complete cycles = 12 days → exactly done.
Answer: 12 days
Answer 12 days
🔑 Find work in 2-day cycle. Multiply → check remainder. If remainder ≤ A's rate, A finishes it on the next day (odd day). If > A's rate, B takes a partial day after.
Q6 A can do a piece of work in 10 days, B in 15 days, C in 20 days. All three start together but A leaves after 2 days and B leaves 3 days before the work is finished. How many days does the work take?
Hard
Solution
LCM(10,15,20) = 60. A=6/day, B=4/day, C=3/day.
Let total days = T.
A works 2 days, B works (T−3) days, C works T days.
Work: 6×2 + 4×(T−3) + 3×T = 60
12 + 4T − 12 + 3T = 60 → 7T = 60 → T = 60/7 ≈ 8.57 days
Answer 60/7 days (≈ 8.57 days)
🔑 Set up total work equation. Each person's contribution = rate × their individual working days. Solve for T.
Q7 12 men can complete a work in 8 days working 6 hours a day. How many men are needed to complete the same work in 4 days working 9 hours a day?
Medium
Solution
Total man-hours = 12 × 8 × 6 = 576.
New scenario: M × 4 × 9 = 576 → M = 576/36 = 16 men
Answer 16 men
🔑 Men × Days × Hours = Total work (man-hours). Constant across both scenarios. Fewer days + fewer hours/day → need more men.
03 Time, Speed & Distance
7 questions▶
📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: Speed × Time = Distance. Every train/boat/race problem is a variation of this.

Relative Speed: Same direction = |S1 − S2|; Opposite = S1 + S2
Train crossing a pole/person: Distance = Length of train
Train crossing a platform: Distance = Length of train + Length of platform
Boats: Downstream = B + R; Upstream = B − R (B=boat speed, R=current)
Average Speed (equal distances): 2S1S2/(S1+S2) ← NOT (S1+S2)/2
Approach for trains:
①Identify what's crossing what → set the correct distance
②Use relative speed if two bodies are involved
③Convert km/hr ↔ m/s: ×5/18 or ×18/5
Q1 A train 150m long running at 72 km/hr crosses a platform in 20 seconds. What is the platform's length?
Easy
Solution
Speed = 72 × 5/18 = 20 m/s.
Distance in 20 sec = 20 × 20 = 400 m.
Platform = 400 − 150 = 250 m
Answer 250 metres
🔑 Always convert km/hr to m/s when time is in seconds. 72 km/hr = 20 m/s is a memorisable pair.
Q2 Two trains 200m and 180m start towards each other from stations 560m apart at speeds 40 km/hr and 30 km/hr. After how long do they meet (from start)?
Hard
Solution
They move toward each other: relative speed = 70 km/hr = 70×(5/18) = 175/9 m/s.
Distance between fronts = 560 m. Time to meet (fronts touch) = 560 ÷ (175/9) = 560×9/175 = 28.8 sec.
Note: "meet" = fronts touch, not fully cross. Answer = 28.8 seconds
Answer 28.8 seconds
🔑 "Meet" ≠ "cross each other." Meeting = fronts touch (distance = gap between them). Crossing = full train passes = gap + both train lengths.
Q3 A boat goes 30 km upstream in 6 hrs and 36 km downstream in 4 hrs. Find the boat's speed in still water.
Medium
Solution
Upstream speed = 30/6 = 5 km/hr → B − R = 5
Downstream speed = 36/4 = 9 km/hr → B + R = 9
Add: 2B = 14 → B = 7 km/hr
Answer 7 km/hr
🔑 Boat speed in still water = (Downstream + Upstream) / 2. Current speed = (Downstream − Upstream) / 2.
Q4 A covers half a journey at 40 km/hr and the rest at 60 km/hr. What is his average speed for the entire journey?
Medium
Solution
Equal distance → use harmonic mean formula: 2S1S2/(S1+S2)
= 2×40×60 / (40+60) = 4800/100 = 48 km/hr
Answer 48 km/hr
🔑 Average speed is NEVER (S1+S2)/2 unless time is equal. For equal distance, use harmonic mean.
Q5 A and B run a 1000m race. A gives B a 100m head start. A beats B by 40 seconds. A's speed is 10 m/s. Find B's speed.
Hard
Solution
A runs 1000m at 10 m/s → A's time = 100 sec.
A beats B by 40 sec → B finishes in 140 sec.
B runs 900m (1000 − 100m head start) in 140 sec → B's speed = 900/140 = 45/7 ≈ 6.43 m/s
Answer 45/7 m/s ≈ 6.43 m/s
🔑 Head start reduces B's distance, not A's. "A beats by 40 sec" means when A finishes, B still has 40s worth of running left.
Q6 A thief runs at 8 km/hr. A policeman 100m behind chases at 10 km/hr. In how many minutes does the policeman catch the thief?
Medium
Solution
Relative speed (same direction) = 10 − 8 = 2 km/hr.
Gap to close = 100m = 0.1 km.
Time = 0.1/2 = 0.05 hr = 0.05 × 60 = 3 minutes
Answer 3 minutes
🔑 Pursuit problems: relative speed = difference of speeds (same direction). Time = initial gap ÷ relative speed.
Q7 A person travelling at 5/6th of his usual speed reaches his destination 10 minutes late. Find his usual time for the journey.
Hard
Solution
Let usual time = T minutes. At 5/6 speed, time = (6/5)T.
Extra time = (6/5)T − T = T/5 = 10 → T = 50 minutes
Answer 50 minutes
🔑 Speed and time are inversely proportional (distance fixed). If speed is 5/6 of normal, time is 6/5 of normal. Excess = T × (1/5) = 10 → T = 50.
04 Profit & Loss
5 questions▶
📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: CP = Cost Price (what you paid). SP = Selling Price (what you got). Profit/Loss is ALWAYS calculated on CP unless stated otherwise.

Profit% = (SP−CP)/CP × 100
SP = CP × (1 + Profit%/100)
Marked Price (MP), Discount on MP → SP = MP × (1 − Discount%/100)
Two items sold at same SP, one x% profit, one x% loss → net = always a LOSS of x²/100 %
Approach:
①Identify CP, SP, and which value the % is applied on
②For successive discounts, use multiplier approach
③Dishonest trader questions: the cheat is in weight — compute effective SP per actual unit
Q1 A trader marks goods 30% above CP and gives 10% discount. What is his profit%?
Easy
Solution
Let CP = 100. MP = 130. Discount 10% → SP = 130 × 0.9 = 117.
Profit = 117 − 100 = 17 → Profit% = 17%
Answer 17%
🔑 Always assume CP = 100 for ease. Multiplier approach: SP = CP × (1+mark%) × (1−discount%).
Q2 Two TVs are sold at ₹9,900 each. One is sold at 10% profit and another at 10% loss. What is the overall gain or loss?
Medium
Solution
Same SP, same %, one profit one loss → always a net LOSS.
Loss% = x²/100 = 10²/100 = 1%
Total SP = 19,800. CP total = 19,800 / 0.99 = 20,000. Loss = ₹200.
Or: CP1 = 9900/1.1 = 9000; CP2 = 9900/0.9 = 11,000. Total CP = 20,000; Total SP = 19,800. Loss = ₹200
Answer Loss of ₹200 (1% loss)
🔑 Same SP, equal % profit + loss → ALWAYS a net loss. Loss = (x/10)² percent. Classic company exam trap.
Q3 A dishonest dealer claims to sell at CP but uses 900g weights instead of 1000g. What is his actual profit%?
Medium
Solution
He sells 900g but charges for 1000g.
Profit on 900g = 100g worth of goods.
Profit% = 100/900 × 100 = 11.11% ≈ 100/9 %
Answer 11.11% (100/9 %)
🔑 Profit% on cheating = (True − False) / False × 100. Base = what he actually gave (false weight), not what he claimed.
Q4 By selling 33 items, a shopkeeper earns a profit equal to the SP of 11 items. What is his profit%?
Hard
Solution
Let SP of 1 item = x. Profit = 11x. SP of 33 items = 33x. CP = 33x − 11x = 22x.
Profit% = 11x/22x × 100 = 50%
Answer 50%
🔑 Key step: CP = Total SP − Profit. Then Profit% = Profit/CP × 100.
Q5 A shopkeeper offers successive discounts of 20% and 10% on an article marked ₹5000. What does the customer pay?
Easy
Solution
SP = 5000 × 0.8 × 0.9 = 5000 × 0.72 = ₹3600
Answer ₹3600
🔑 Successive discounts are NOT additive (20+10 ≠ 30%). Multiply multipliers: 0.8 × 0.9 = 0.72 → 28% effective discount.
05 Averages
5 questions▶
📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: Average = Sum / Count. Every average problem is really a sum problem in disguise — find the total, then adjust.

Average = Sum of all items / Number of items
New Average after adding item X to group of n: (Old Sum + X) / (n+1)
If average increases by d after adding X: X = Old Average + d×(n+1)
Weighted Average = (w1×a1 + w2×a2) / (w1 + w2)
Average of first n natural numbers = (n+1)/2
Average of first n odd numbers = n; Average of first n even numbers = n+1
Approach:
①Convert average → total sum (Average × Count = Sum)
②Adjust the sum based on what changes (person joins/leaves, wrong entry corrected)
③Recompute average from new sum and new count
Key trap: "Average increases by 2 when a new member joins" — the new member contributes not just their own value but also raises the entire group's average. Set up: (Old Sum + X) / (n+1) = Old Avg + 2.
Q1 Average of 5 numbers is 27. If one number is excluded, the average becomes 25. What is the excluded number?
Easy
Solution
Total of 5 numbers = 5 × 27 = 135
Total of 4 remaining = 4 × 25 = 100
Excluded number = 135 − 100 = 35
Answer 35
🔑 Sum method — always convert averages to sums first, then subtract.
Q2 A batsman's average after 10 innings is 40. In his 11th innings he scores 84. What is his new average?
Easy
Solution
Old sum = 10 × 40 = 400
New sum = 400 + 84 = 484
New average = 484 / 11 = 44
Answer 44
🔑 Classic incremental average. Always recompute from sum, not from "how much above the old average he scored."
Q3 The average weight of a class of 30 students is 40 kg. When the teacher joins, the average becomes 41 kg. What is the teacher's weight?
Medium
Solution
Old sum = 30 × 40 = 1200
New sum = 31 × 41 = 1271
Teacher's weight = 1271 − 1200 = 71 kg
Answer 71 kg
🔑 Shortcut: Teacher's weight = New average + n × (New average − Old average) = 41 + 30×1 = 71. New person always = New average + (group size × change in average).
Q4 In a class of 40 students, the average marks of boys is 70 and girls is 55. The class average is 65. Find the number of boys and girls.
Medium
Solution
Let boys = b, girls = 40 − b
70b + 55(40 − b) = 65 × 40
70b + 2200 − 55b = 2600
15b = 400 → b = 400/15 → Hmm, use alligation instead:
Boys : Girls = (65−55) : (70−65) = 10 : 5 = 2 : 1
Boys = 40 × 2/3 ≈ 27 boys, 13 girls
Answer 27 boys, 13 girls (ratio 2:1)
🔑 Weighted average + alligation combo. Alligation: ratio = (class avg − girls avg) : (boys avg − class avg).
Q5 Average of 11 results is 50. Average of first 6 is 49 and last 6 is 52. What is the 6th result?
Hard
Solution
Total (11) = 550
First 6 sum = 294; Last 6 sum = 312
First 6 + Last 6 = 606 — but this double counts the 6th result
6th result = 606 − 550 = 56
Answer 56
🔑 Classic overlap trap. First 6 + Last 6 = All 11 + 6th (counted twice). So 6th = (Sum of first 6 + Sum of last 6) − Total.
06 Permutations & Combinations
7 questions▶
📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: Permutation = Order matters (arrangement). Combination = Order doesn't matter (selection).

nPr = n! / (n−r)! [arrangements of r from n]
nCr = n! / [r!(n−r)!] [selections of r from n]
nCr = nC(n−r) ← symmetry property
Circular arrangement: (n−1)! [one position fixed]
Identical objects: n! / (p! × q! ...) for repeated items
Decision tree:
①Does order matter? Yes → P, No → C
②Any restrictions? Handle restricted items first (fix them in/out)
③Complement principle: sometimes easier to count "what we DON'T want" and subtract from total
Keyword signals: "arrange" → P; "select/choose/team" → C; "around a table" → circular P; "at least one" → complement.
Q1 In how many ways can the letters of "MISSISSIPPI" be arranged?
Medium
Solution
Letters: M=1, I=4, S=4, P=2. Total = 11 letters.
Arrangements = 11! / (1! × 4! × 4! × 2!) = 39,916,800 / (24 × 24 × 2) = 39,916,800 / 1152 = 34,650
Answer 34,650
🔑 For words with repeated letters: Total! / (product of each letter's frequency factorial).
Q2 A committee of 5 must be formed from 6 men and 4 women such that at least 2 women are included. How many ways?
Hard
Solution
Case 1: 2W + 3M = C(4,2) × C(6,3) = 6 × 20 = 120
Case 2: 3W + 2M = C(4,3) × C(6,2) = 4 × 15 = 60
Case 3: 4W + 1M = C(4,4) × C(6,1) = 1 × 6 = 6
Total = 120 + 60 + 6 = 186
Answer 186 ways
🔑 "At least 2" → enumerate each valid case and add. Or: Total C(10,5) − (0W + 1W cases). Both methods must give 186.
Q3 8 people sit around a circular table. In how many ways can they sit such that 2 specific people are always together?
Medium
Solution
Treat the 2 together as 1 unit → 7 units around circle = (7−1)! = 720 ways.
The 2 within the unit can swap = 2! = 2 ways.
Total = 720 × 2 = 1440
Answer 1440
🔑 Circular: fix one → (n−1)! for others. "Always together" → bundle them, then multiply by internal arrangements.
Q4 How many 4-digit numbers can be formed using digits 1–9 (no repetition) that are divisible by 4?
Hard
Solution
Divisible by 4 → last 2 digits form a number divisible by 4.
From {1–9}, pairs divisible by 4: 12,16,24,28,32,36,48,52,56,64,68,72,76,84,92,96 → 16 valid pairs.
For each pair, remaining 2 digits from 7 remaining digits = 7 × 6 = 42 ways.
Total = 16 × 42 = 672
Answer 672
🔑 Divisibility rule for 4: only last 2 digits matter. List valid last-2-digit pairs first, then fill remaining positions.
Q5 From a group of 7 boys and 5 girls, in how many ways can a team of 4 be chosen with NO girls?
Easy
Solution
All 4 from boys: C(7,4) = 35
Answer 35 ways
🔑 "No girls" simply means all selections from boys only.
Q6 In how many ways can the letters of the word "MISSISSIPPI" be arranged?
Hard
Solution
Total letters = 11. Repeated: M=1, I=4, S=4, P=2.
Arrangements = 11! / (1! × 4! × 4! × 2!)
= 39916800 / (1 × 24 × 24 × 2) = 39916800 / 1152 = 34650
Answer 34,650
🔑 Arrangements with repeats: total! ÷ (count of each repeated letter)!. Always count repeats carefully — MISSISSIPPI has 4 I's and 4 S's, not 3.
Q7 From a group of 7 men and 5 women, a committee of 6 is formed such that it has at least 2 women. In how many ways?
Hard
Solution
"At least 2 women" = exactly 2W + exactly 3W + exactly 4W + exactly 5W
2W,4M: C(5,2)×C(7,4) = 10×35 = 350
3W,3M: C(5,3)×C(7,3) = 10×35 = 350
4W,2M: C(5,4)×C(7,2) = 5×21 = 105
5W,1M: C(5,5)×C(7,1) = 1×7 = 7
Total = 350+350+105+7 = 812
Answer 812
🔑 "At least" = enumerate all valid cases and sum. Alternative: Total C(12,6) − (0 women) − (1 woman) = 924 − C(7,6) − C(5,1)×C(7,5) = 924 − 7 − 105 = 812. Complement method is faster here.
07 Linear & Quadratic Equations
5 questions▶
📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: Every word problem in aptitude eventually becomes an equation. Learn to translate words → algebra fast.

Linear: ax + b = 0 → x = −b/a
Simultaneous: Substitution or Elimination method
Quadratic: ax² + bx + c = 0
Roots: x = [−b ± √(b²−4ac)] / 2a
Sum of roots = −b/a; Product of roots = c/a
Discriminant (b²−4ac): >0 → 2 real roots; =0 → 1 root; <0 → no real roots
Approach for word problems:
①Assign variables to unknowns (usually 1 variable if possible)
②Translate each condition into an equation
③Solve, then verify both roots make physical sense (no negative ages, etc.)
Factoring shortcut: For ax² + bx + c, find two numbers that multiply to ac and add to b. Split the middle term.
Q1 The sum of two numbers is 45 and their difference is 9. Find the numbers.
Easy
Solution
x + y = 45, x − y = 9
Add: 2x = 54 → x = 27, y = 18
Answer 27 and 18
🔑 Sum + difference problems: larger = (Sum + Diff)/2, smaller = (Sum − Diff)/2. No need to set up formal equations.
Q2 The product of two consecutive even numbers is 168. Find the numbers.
Medium
Solution
n(n+2) = 168 → n² + 2n − 168 = 0
Discriminant: 4 + 672 = 676 = 26²
n = (−2 + 26)/2 = 12
Numbers = 12 and 14
Answer 12 and 14
🔑 For quick approximation: √168 ≈ 13, so consecutive evens near 13 → try 12×14 = 168. ✓ Intuition beats formula speed.
Q3 If α and β are roots of x² − 5x + 6 = 0, find α² + β².
Medium
Solution
α + β = 5 (sum of roots = −b/a)
αβ = 6 (product of roots = c/a)
α² + β² = (α+β)² − 2αβ = 25 − 12 = 13
Answer 13
🔑 α² + β² = (α+β)² − 2αβ. Memorize this identity — it appears constantly in Infosys and TCS papers.
Q4 A two-digit number is 7 times the sum of its digits. If 27 is subtracted, the digits reverse. Find the number.
Hard
Solution
Number = 10a + b. Condition 1: 10a + b = 7(a + b) → 3a = 6b → a = 2b
Condition 2: 10a + b − 27 = 10b + a → 9a − 9b = 27 → a − b = 3
a = 2b and a − b = 3 → b = 3, a = 6
Number = 63
Answer 63
🔑 Two-digit number template: always write as 10a + b where a = tens digit, b = units digit. Reversed = 10b + a.
Q5 For what value of k does kx² + 4x + 1 = 0 have equal roots?
Medium
Solution
Equal roots → Discriminant = 0
b² − 4ac = 0 → 16 − 4k = 0 → k = 4
Answer k = 4
🔑 Equal roots always means D = 0. Two real distinct roots: D > 0. No real roots: D < 0. This mapping appears in every major recruitment paper.

08 Clocks & Calendars
5 questions▶
📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: Clocks are circular tracks; calendars are modular arithmetic with 7-day cycles.

Clock: Hour hand speed = 0.5°/min, Minute hand = 6°/min. Relative speed = 5.5°/min.
Angle between hands = |30H − 5.5M| (take min with 360−θ)
Hands coincide every 65 5/11 minutes (12 times in 12 hours)
Hands opposite = 180° apart → 11 times in 12 hours
Calendar: Odd days = remainder when days ÷ 7. 100 years = 5 odd days, 400 years = 0 odd days.
Approach:
①For clock angles: compute absolute position of each hand, find difference, take minimum arc
②For "when will they": use relative speed concept (fast − slow)
③For calendars: count total odd days from reference date (usually Sunday=0)
Calendar anchors: 1 Jan 1900 = Monday. 1 Jan 2000 = Saturday. 1 Jan 2024 = Monday.
Q1 At what time between 3:00 and 4:00 will the hour and minute hands of a clock be exactly opposite each other?
Medium
Solution
At 3:00, the minute hand is at 0° and the hour hand is at 90°. Initial gap = 90°.
To be opposite (180° apart), the minute hand must first gain 90° to coincide with the hour hand, and then gain an additional 180° to move ahead. Total relative gain needed = 90° + 180° = 270°.
Relative speed = 5.5°/min. Time = 270 / 5.5 = 540/11 = 49 1/11 minutes past 3.
Answer 3:49 1/11 or 3:54.545...
🔑 Opposite = 180° apart. At H:00, hands are at 30H° apart. Need to cover (180 − 30H) or (180 + 30H) degrees.
Q2 What is the angle between clock hands at 4:20?
Easy
Solution
Hour hand: 4 × 30 + 20 × 0.5 = 120 + 10 = 130°.
Minute hand: 20 × 6 = 120°.
Difference = |130 − 120| = 10°.
Answer 10°
🔑 Hour hand moves! At 4:20, it's not at 4 exactly — it's 1/3 of the way to 5 (20 min = 1/3 hour, so 10° extra).
Q3 A clock loses 5 minutes every hour. It was set correctly at 12 noon on Monday. What time will it show when actual time is 12 noon on Wednesday?
Hard
Solution
Actual time elapsed = 48 hours.
Clock runs at 55 min per actual 60 min (loses 5).

Clock shows 8:00 AM on Tuesday when it's actually Wednesday noon.
Answer 8:00 AM Tuesday (44 hours elapsed on clock)
🔑 Defective clocks: Find ratio of clock time to real time, then apply. Losing time → clock shows less elapsed.
Q4 What day of the week was 15 August 1947 (Indian Independence Day)?
Medium
Solution

Q5 How many times do clock hands form a right angle (90°) in 12 hours?
Easy
Solution
Hands at 90°: minute hand 90° ahead or behind hour hand.
Relative speed = 5.5°/min. Time between 90° positions = 180/5.5 = 360/11 min.
In 12 hours (720 min): 720 ÷ (360/11) = 22 times.
But between 2-3 and 8-9, the 90° happens 3 times each (not 2), so total is 22 times (11 times per 6 hours, but 2-3 and 8-9 have overlaps that cancel out).
Actually: 22 times in 12 hours (not 24, because at 3:00 and 9:00 exact, it's counted once per hour position but happens in transition).
Answer 22 times
🔑 In 12 hours: coincide 11 times, opposite 11 times, at right angles 22 times. In 24 hours: 22, 11, and 44 respectively (coincide/opposite don't double because 11th/12th are same).
09 Progressions (AP/GP/HP)
5 questions▶
📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: AP = linear growth, GP = exponential growth, HP = reciprocal AP (rates, time-work).

AP: nth term = a + (n−1)d; Sum = n/2 × [2a + (n−1)d] = n/2 × (first + last)
GP: nth term = ar^(n−1); Sum (r≠1) = a(r^n − 1)/(r−1); Infinite sum (|r| <1)=a/(1−r)
HP: nth term = 1/(a + (n−1)d); Means: AM ≥ GM ≥ HM for positive reals
Special: Sum of first n natural numbers = n(n+1)/2; Sum of squares = n(n+1)(2n+1)/6; Sum of cubes = [n(n+1)/2]²
Approach:
①Identify progression type from context (constant difference → AP, constant ratio → GP, rates/time → HP)
②For AP/GP sums: identify a, d/r, n clearly before applying formula
③For means: AM×HM = GM² = a×b (for two numbers)
Traps: "Between" means exclusive (not including endpoints). "Including" means n+2 terms between two numbers.
Q1 Find the sum of all two-digit numbers divisible by 3.
Medium
Solution
First two-digit ÷3: 12. Last: 99. AP with a=12, d=3, last=99.
n: 12 + (n−1)×3 = 99 → (n−1)×3 = 87 → n = 30.
Sum = 30/2 × (12 + 99) = 15 × 111 = 1665.
Answer 1665
🔑 Two-digit constraint is inclusive (10-99). First multiple of 3 ≥10 is 12, not 3.
Q2 The 4th and 10th terms of an AP are 13 and 25 respectively. Find the 20th term.
Easy
Solution
a + 3d = 13; a + 9d = 25.
Subtract: 6d = 12 → d = 2. Then a = 13 − 6 = 7.
20th term = 7 + 19×2 = 7 + 38 = 45.
Answer 45
🔑 Two equations, two unknowns (a and d). Solve simultaneously, then apply nth term formula.
Q3 A GP has first term 3 and sum to infinity 12. Find the common ratio.
Medium
Solution
S∞ = a/(1−r) = 12, a = 3.
3/(1−r) = 12 → 1−r = 3/12 = 1/4 → r = 3/4.
Answer r = 3/4
🔑 |r| < 1 required for convergent infinite GP. If |r| ≥ 1, sum diverges (infinite).
Q4 The AM and GM of two positive numbers are 25 and 15 respectively. Find their HM.
Hard
Solution
For two numbers: AM × HM = GM².
25 × HM = 15² = 225 → HM = 225/25 = 9.
Answer 9
🔑 AM ≥ GM ≥ HM always. For two numbers: GM² = AM×HM. This saves solving for actual numbers.
Q5 Insert 4 harmonic means between 1/2 and 1/12.
Hard
Solution
HP means reciprocals form AP. Reciprocals: 2 and 12.
AP with 6 terms total (2 ends + 4 means): a=2, l=12, n=6.
12 = 2 + 5d → d = 2.
AP terms: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12.
HP terms (reciprocals): 1/2, 1/4, 1/6, 1/8, 1/10, 1/12.
4 HM: 1/4, 1/6, 1/8, 1/10.
Answer 1/4, 1/6, 1/8, 1/10
🔑 HP is always solved via AP of reciprocals. "Insert n means" → total n+2 terms in the progression.

11 Syllogisms
5 questions▶
📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: Draw Venn diagrams mentally. Conclusions must be 100% true based on the given statements — not "maybe true."

All A are B → A circle inside B circle
No A is B → A and B circles don't overlap
Some A are B → A and B overlap partially
Some A are not B → part of A is outside B
Rules:
①Two particular (Some) premises → no definite conclusion
②Two negative premises → no definite conclusion
③"All A are B" + "All B are C" → "All A are C" (chain rule)
④Check ALL diagram possibilities — if even one case makes conclusion false, conclusion doesn't follow
Q1 Statements: All cats are dogs. All dogs are animals. Conclusion I: All cats are animals. II: Some animals are cats. Which follow?
Easy
Solution
All cats are dogs + All dogs are animals → All cats are animals (Conclusion I ✓)
If all cats are animals, then some animals are cats (conversion: All A are B → Some B are A) ✓
Both I and II follow.
Answer Both I and II follow
🔑 "All A are B" always implies "Some B are A." This conversion rule catches many students off guard.
Q2 Statements: Some pens are books. No book is a pencil. Conclusions: I. Some pens are not pencils. II. No pen is a pencil.
Medium
Solution
Some pens = books. No book = pencil. → Those pens that are books are definitely not pencils.
So: Some pens are not pencils. (I ✓)
But we don't know about pens that are NOT books — they could be pencils. So II is not definite. (II ✗)
Answer Only I follows
🔑 "Some pens are books" doesn't tell us about ALL pens. Never extend a "some" to "all/no".
Q3 Statements: No teacher is a student. All students are intelligent. Conclusions: I. No teacher is intelligent. II. Some intelligent are not teachers.
Hard
Solution
No teacher is a student. All students are intelligent.
Students are a subset of intelligent. Teachers ∩ Students = ∅.
But teachers could still be intelligent (just not through the student path).
Conclusion I: Cannot conclude — teachers might or might not be intelligent. (I ✗)
Conclusion II: All students are intelligent, and no student is a teacher → those students (intelligent but not teachers) exist. So some intelligent are not teachers. (II ✓)
Answer Only II follows
🔑 Draw it: Students ⊂ Intelligent. Teachers ∩ Students = ∅. Intelligent circle extends beyond students — could include teachers too. So I is not certain.
Q4 Statements: All A are B. Some B are C. Conclusion: Some A are C. Does it follow?
Medium
Solution
All A are B → A is completely inside B.
Some B are C → part of B overlaps with C. But that overlap might not include the A region.
It's POSSIBLE that some A are C (if the B∩C overlap includes A), but NOT certain. Does not follow.
Answer Does NOT follow
🔑 All A are B + Some B are C → the "some" might be in the non-A part of B. Classic trap. Only "Some B are C" and "All A are B" doesn't guarantee A meets C.
Q5 Statements: Some fruits are vegetables. All vegetables are plants. No plant is a tree. Conclusion: No fruit is a tree.
Hard
Solution
All vegetables → plants. No plant → tree. So: No vegetable is a tree. ✓
Some fruits are vegetables → those fruits are plants → not trees. (Some fruits are not trees.)
But other fruits (not vegetables) could be trees. So "No fruit is a tree" is NOT certain. Does not follow.
Answer Does NOT follow (only "Some fruits are not trees" can be concluded)
🔑 "Some" in premises limits conclusions to "some." You can never universally extend a "some" premise to reach a "no/all" conclusion.
12 Statement, Assumptions & Conclusions
5 questions▶
📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: Syllogisms are deductive — you derive what MUST follow. This topic is evaluative — you judge whether a given assumption or conclusion is implicit in a statement. The bar is different: not "must be true" but "reasonably implied."

Assumption: An unstated premise that must be true for the statement to make sense
Conclusion: Something that logically follows from the statement as a direct result
Course of action: A practical step that follows from the problem stated
Inference: A judgment drawn from the facts given

A valid assumption: fills a logical gap in the statement, is not too far-fetched, does not restate the obvious
Rules:
①Assumptions must be implicit (not stated, but required). If it's stated in the sentence, it's not an assumption.
②Conclusions must follow directly. If it requires extra knowledge or a leap, it doesn't follow.
③Courses of action must be practical and directly address the problem in the statement.
Q1 Statement: "Put up a notice board in the office to improve internal communication." Assumption I: Staff read notice boards. Assumption II: Communication in the office is poor. Are these assumptions valid?
Medium
Solution
Assumption I: "Staff read notice boards" — This MUST be assumed for the suggestion to make sense. If no one reads the board, the action is pointless. ✓ Valid.
Assumption II: "Communication is poor" — The word "improve" in the statement implies current communication is lacking. This is a reasonable implicit assumption. ✓ Valid.
Both assumptions are implicit.
Answer Both assumptions I and II are implicit
🔑 Any recommendation assumes the problem it addresses exists. "Improve X" assumes X is currently inadequate. The action also assumes it will work, meaning people will use it.
Q2 Statement: "If it rains tomorrow, the match will be cancelled." It rained tomorrow. Conclusion I: The match was cancelled. Conclusion II: The players were disappointed.
Easy
Solution
Conclusion I: Rain → match cancelled. Rain occurred → match cancelled. ✓ Follows directly.
Conclusion II: "Players were disappointed" — requires knowing players wanted to play. The statement says nothing about player feelings. ✗ Does not follow.
Answer Only Conclusion I follows
🔑 A conclusion must follow from the statement alone — no outside assumptions allowed. Disappointment requires emotional inference not supported by the statement.
Q3 Statement: "Many students fail in mathematics due to lack of practice." Course of Action I: Students should practice math daily. Course of Action II: Math should be removed from the syllabus.
Easy
Solution
Course I: "Practice daily" — directly addresses the stated cause (lack of practice). ✓ Follows.
Course II: "Remove from syllabus" — removing math doesn't address the problem of practice; it avoids it. This is extreme and doesn't solve the root cause. ✗ Does not follow.
Answer Only Course of Action I follows
🔑 A valid course of action must directly solve the problem stated — not avoid, circumvent, or over-correct it. Removing a subject for poor scores = over-reaction, not solution.
Q4 Statement: "Advertisements are published to increase sales." Assumption I: People buy products after seeing ads. Assumption II: Sales are currently low.
Medium
Solution
Assumption I: Ads influencing purchases is the ENTIRE basis of advertising. Without this assumption, the statement makes no sense. ✓ Valid.
Assumption II: "Sales are currently low" — the statement says ads are published TO INCREASE sales. This doesn't imply sales are low; companies advertise to grow from any level. ✗ Not necessarily implicit.
Answer Only Assumption I is implicit
🔑 "Increase" doesn't mean "currently low." A healthy company still advertises to grow further. Don't read extra negative implications into neutral statements.
Q5 Statement: "The government has decided to make primary education free." Conclusion I: More children will now attend school. Conclusion II: The quality of education will improve.
Hard
Solution
Conclusion I: Free education removes a financial barrier → more families can send children to school. This is a reasonable, direct consequence. ✓ Follows.
Conclusion II: Free doesn't mean better quality — these are independent variables. The statement says nothing about quality improvements. ✗ Does not follow.
Answer Only Conclusion I follows
🔑 "Free" affects access (quantity), not quality. Never conflate affordability with quality — examiners exploit this confusion deliberately.

16 Input-Output / Machine Coding
5 questions▶
📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: A machine applies the same operation repeatedly at each step. Your job is to find the rule from the given input-output steps, then apply it forward or backward.

Common operation types:
Shift: each word/number shifts left or right by a fixed amount
Sort: numbers arrange in ascending/descending order step by step
Swap: specific positions swap each step
Replace: words replaced by their positional number or vice versa
Combine: shift + sort applied alternately
Approach:
①Compare Step 0 (input) to Step 1 — what changed? Note positions of what moved.
②Compare Step 1 to Step 2 — does the same rule apply? Confirm the pattern.
③Apply confirmed rule to reach the required step, or work backward if the final state is given.
Key trap: Some machines alternate between two rules (odd steps vs even steps). Always verify at least 3 steps before committing to a single rule.
Q1 Input: 5 3 8 1 6 2. Step 1: 1 3 8 5 6 2. Step 2: 1 2 8 5 6 3. Step 3: 1 2 3 5 6 8. What is the rule and how many steps to complete?
Medium
Solution
Step 0→1: Smallest number (1) moves to leftmost position.
Step 1→2: Next smallest (2) moves to 2nd position.
Step 2→3: Next (3) moves to 3rd. Pattern: selection sort — each step places the next smallest number in order.
6 numbers → 5 steps to complete (last number auto-sorts).
Answer Selection sort — 5 steps to complete
🔑 Selection sort pattern: each step fixes one more element from the left. n numbers → (n−1) steps. This is the most common input-output pattern in Cognizant and Capgemini papers.
Q2 Input: mango apple kiwi banana. Step 1: apple mango kiwi banana. Step 2: apple banana kiwi mango. Step 3: apple banana kiwi mango. What is Step 4?
Medium
Solution
Step 0→1: Words sort alphabetically from left (apple comes before mango).
Step 1→2: banana placed in sorted position.
Step 2→3: Already sorted — no change.
Step 3 = Step 4 (machine halts when sorted): apple banana kiwi mango
Answer apple banana kiwi mango (no change — already sorted)
🔑 When a step produces no change, the machine has reached its final state. All subsequent steps are identical to that step.
Q3 Input: 12 45 7 23 56 3. If the rule is "move the largest number to the end each step," what does Step 2 look like?
Easy
Solution
Input: 12 45 7 23 56 3
Step 1: Largest (56) moves to end → 12 45 7 23 3 56
Step 2: Largest of remaining (45) moves to end (before 56) → 12 7 23 3 45 56
Answer 12 7 23 3 45 56
🔑 Bubble sort variant: each step locks the largest remaining element into its final position from the right. Already-sorted tail stays fixed.
Q4 Input: @4 #9 $2 %7. Rule: symbols shift one position right (last wraps to first), numbers stay. Step 1: %4 @9 #2 $7. What is Step 3?
Hard
Solution
Input: @4 #9 $2 %7
Step 1: %4 @9 #2 $7 (symbols rotate right)
Step 2: $4 %9 @2 #7 (rotate right again)
Step 3: #4 $9 %2 @7 (rotate right once more)
Answer #4 $9 %2 @7
🔑 Rotation problems: track one element to confirm the cycle length. % was in position 4, went to position 1 (Step 1), position 2 (Step 2), position 3 (Step 3). After 4 steps it returns to original — cycle = 4.

📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: Track movement on 2D plane. Use coordinate system or visual tracing. Final displacement uses Pythagoras or relative positioning.

Standard: North (+y), East (+x), South (−y), West (−x)
Left turn from North = West; Right turn from North = East
Left/Right is relative to current facing direction
Shadow direction: Morning (sunrise) → shadow West; Noon → shadow minimal; Evening → shadow East
Approach:
①Start at origin (0,0), facing North (default unless stated)
②Process each move: straight = add to current facing vector; turn = change facing direction only
③For "how far from start": √(Δx² + Δy²). For direction: tan⁻¹(Δy/Δx) referenced to axes
Shadow rule: Sun rises East, sets West. Morning shadow points West; evening shadow points East.

Turn right (from South) → facing West. Walk 3 km → (−3, −5).
Turn right (from West) → facing North. Walk 5 km → (−3, 0).
Distance from (0,0) = √(9 + 0) = 3 km.
Answer 3 km
🔑 Right turn from South = West. Right turn from West = North. Track coordinates after each move.
Q2 At sunrise, a man shadows himself towards his left. Which direction is he facing?
Medium
Solution
Sunrise = sun in East → shadow points West.
Shadow is on his left → his left side is West.
If left = West, then facing South (North would have left=West? No: facing North, left=West. ✓).
Answer North
🔑 Facing North: Left=West, Right=East. Sunrise shadow West → matches left side. He faces North.
Q3 A man travels 10 km North, turns left, travels 5 km, turns left, travels 10 km. How far and in what direction is he from start?
Medium
Solution
(0,0) → 10 North → (0, 10), facing North.
Left turn → West. 5 km → (−5, 10).
Left turn → South. 10 km → (−5, 0).
Final position (−5, 0). Distance = 5 km. Direction = 5 km West.

🔑 Left from North = West; Left from West = South. The path forms a U-shape, ending directly west of start.

Start (0,0), North.

Morning = sun East → shadow West.

18 Data Sufficiency
5 questions▶
📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: Don't solve the problem — determine if you CAN solve it. Test each statement independently, then together.

Answer choices (standard):
(A) Statement I alone sufficient, II not
(B) Statement II alone sufficient, I not
(C) Both together sufficient, neither alone
(D) Each statement alone sufficient
(E) Neither sufficient, even together
Approach:
①Read question carefully. Identify exactly what needs to be found (specific value? yes/no?).
②Analyze Statement I alone: can you answer? If yes → (A) or (D). If no → (B), (C), or (E).
③Analyze Statement II alone: same logic. If both yes → (D). If II yes and I no → (B).
④If both no individually: combine. Do they together provide enough? Yes → (C), No → (E).
Critical: Never assume facts from one statement when testing the other alone. Don't calculate the actual answer — just determine sufficiency.
Q1 What is the area of rectangle ABCD?
I. Length is 12 cm.
II. Diagonal is 13 cm.
Easy
Solution
Need: Length × Breadth.
I alone: Only length known. Insufficient.
II alone: Only diagonal. Could be any 5-12-13 or other right triangle. Insufficient.
I + II: l=12, d=13. By Pythagoras: b² = 13² − 12² = 169 − 144 = 25 → b=5.
Area = 12×5 = 60. Both together sufficient. Answer: (C).
Answer (C) — Both statements together are sufficient
🔑 Area needs two dimensions. Diagonal + one side gives the other via Pythagoras. Classic C-type problem.
Q2 Is x > y?
I. x² > y²
II. x³ > y³
Medium
Solution
I: x² > y² → |x| > |y|. Could be x=3,y=2 (yes) or x=−3,y=2 (no). Insufficient.
II: x³ > y³. Since f(t)=t³ is strictly increasing, this means x > y. Sufficient alone.
Answer: (B).
Answer (B) — Statement II alone is sufficient
🔑 Odd powers preserve sign and order; even powers lose sign information. x³ > y³ ⇔ x > y always.
Q3 What is the value of x + y?
I. x² − y² = 16
II. x − y = 2
Medium
Solution
I: (x+y)(x−y) = 16. One equation, two unknowns. Insufficient alone.
II: x−y = 2. Insufficient alone (infinite solutions).
I + II: (x+y)×2 = 16 → x+y = 8. Sufficient together.
Answer: (C).
Answer (C) — Both together sufficient
🔑 Difference of squares factorization is key. I gives product, II gives one factor — together yield the other factor directly.
Q4 How old is Rahul?
I. Rahul is 5 years older than his brother.
II. Rahul's brother is 20 years old now.
Easy
Solution
I alone: R = B + 5, but B unknown. Insufficient.
II alone: B = 20, but relation to R unknown. Insufficient.
I + II: R = 20 + 5 = 25. Sufficient together.
Answer: (C).
Answer (C) — Both together sufficient
🔑 Classic two-variable setup. Each statement gives one equation; need both to solve.
Q5 Is integer n divisible by 6?
I. n is divisible by 3.
II. n is divisible by 2.
Hard
Solution
Divisible by 6 = divisible by both 2 and 3 (since 2,3 coprime).
I alone: n=3 (no) or n=6 (yes). Insufficient.
II alone: n=2 (no) or n=6 (yes). Insufficient.
I + II: n divisible by both 2 and 3 → n divisible by 6. Sufficient together.
Answer: (C).
Answer (C) — Both together sufficient
🔑 For coprime factors, divisibility by product requires divisibility by each factor. 2 and 3 are coprime, so LCM(2,3)=6.

Sentence Correction — What to check (in order):
①Subject-verb agreement (singular subject → singular verb)
②Tense consistency throughout the sentence
③Pronoun agreement (his/her/their match the subject)
④Modifiers next to the thing they modify
⑤Parallelism (items in a list use same grammatical form)

Para Jumbles — Approach:
Step 1: Find the opening sentence (introduces topic, no pronoun reference to prior text)
Step 2: Find the closing sentence (summary/conclusion/result)
Step 3: Find pairs/chains — sentence with "this/these/it" follows its referent

Q1 Identify the error: "Each of the students have submitted their assignment on time."
Easy
Solution
"Each of the students" → subject = "Each" (singular). Verb should be "has" not "have."
Also "their" should be "his/her" for strict agreement, though modern usage allows "their."
Primary error: "have" should be "has". Corrected: "Each of the students has submitted their assignment on time."
Answer "have" → "has" (subject-verb agreement)
🔑 "Each," "every," "either," "neither" are singular. Don't be fooled by "of the students" — the subject is "Each," not "students."
Q2 Identify the error: "She is one of those managers who takes pride in their team's success."
Medium
Solution
"One of those managers who..." — the antecedent of "who" is "managers" (plural), not "one."
So the verb should be plural: "take" not "takes."
Corrected: "...managers who take pride..."
Answer "takes" → "take"
🔑 "One of those X who/that..." → verb agrees with X (plural), not "one." This is a high-frequency trap in TCS/Infosys tests.
Q3 Para Jumble: Arrange P,Q,R,S in order. P: It also reduces carbon emissions significantly. Q: Solar energy is becoming increasingly cost-competitive. R: Governments worldwide are now incentivizing its adoption. S: This makes it a viable solution to the climate crisis.
Medium
Solution
Q introduces the topic (no prior reference) → opener.

S: "This makes it a viable solution" — "this" refers to cost-competitiveness + emissions reduction (Q+P). S follows Q+P.
R: "Governments are incentivizing" — this is a consequence of S (it's viable → governments act). R = closing.
Order: Q → P → S → R
Answer Q P S R
🔑 "Also" = addition (not an opener). "This/these/it" refers back to something just said. "Therefore/thus" = conclusion. Use these as connectors to chain sentences.

Solution

21 Set Theory & Venn Diagrams
5 questions▶
📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: Visualize overlaps. Use n(A∪B∪C) formula for three sets. The "exactly one," "exactly two," "all three" breakdown is essential.

Two sets: n(A∪B) = n(A) + n(B) − n(A∩B)
Three sets: n(A∪B∪C) = n(A)+n(B)+n(C) − n(A∩B)−n(B∩C)−n(C∩A) + n(A∩B∩C)
Exactly one = a + b + c (only A, only B, only C)
Exactly two = d + e + f (A∩B only, B∩C only, C∩A only)
All three = g
Total = Exactly one + Exactly two + All three + Neither
Approach:
①Draw the diagram. Label regions: only A, only B, only C, A∩B only, B∩C only, C∩A only, all three, neither.
②Start filling from innermost (all three) or from "only" regions if given.
③"At least two" = (exactly two) + (all three). "At least one" = total − neither.
Maximum value: n(A∩B) ≤ min(n(A), n(B)). Minimum: n(A)+n(B) − total (if forced overlap).
Q1 In a survey of 100 students: 60 like tea, 50 like coffee, 30 like both. How many like neither?
Easy
Solution
n(T∪C) = n(T) + n(C) − n(T∩C) = 60 + 50 − 30 = 80.
Neither = Total − n(T∪C) = 100 − 80 = 20.
Answer 20 students
🔑 Classic two-set formula. Union = at least one. Neither = total minus union.
Q2 In a company: 80 speak English, 60 speak Hindi, 40 speak both. Total employees = 120. How many speak only English?
Medium
Solution
Only English = n(E) − n(E∩H) = 80 − 40 = 40.
(Verify: Only Hindi = 60−40=20; Both=40; Neither=120−(40+20+40)=20. Checks out.)
Answer 40 employees
🔑 "Only English" is not n(E) — it's n(E) minus those who also speak Hindi (the intersection).
Q3 In a group of 70 people: 37 like coffee, 52 like tea, and everyone likes at least one. How many like both?
Medium

n(C∪T) = n(C) + n(T) − n(C∩T)
70 = 37 + 52 − Both → 70 = 89 − Both → Both = 19.

🔑 "Everyone likes at least one" means n(C∪T) = total. Rearrange formula to solve for intersection.
Q4 In a survey of 200 people: 100 like tea, 80 like coffee, 60 like milk. 40 like tea and coffee, 30 like coffee and milk, 20 like tea and milk, 10 like all three. How many like exactly one beverage?
Hard
Solution
Tea only = ∣𝑇∣ − ∣𝑇∩𝐶∣ − ∣𝑇∩𝑀∣ + ∣𝑇∩𝐶∩𝑀∣

Core Idea: Indices are shorthand for repeated multiplication. Surds are irrational roots that can't be simplified to whole numbers. Both follow the same handful of laws — memorize them, don't derive on the fly.

aᵐ × aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ
aᵐ ÷ aⁿ = aᵐ⁻ⁿ
(aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ
a⁰ = 1 (any non-zero base)
a⁻ⁿ = 1/aⁿ
a^(1/n) = ⁿ√a
a^(m/n) = ⁿ√(aᵐ)
(ab)ⁿ = aⁿbⁿ
Rationalizing: multiply numerator and denominator by conjugate (√a − √b) to remove surd from denominator
Approach:
①Convert all terms to the same base if possible (8 = 2³, 27 = 3³, 32 = 2⁵)
②Apply index laws to simplify — add/subtract exponents when bases match
③For surds in denominators: rationalize using conjugate before comparing
Key base conversions to memorize: 4=2², 8=2³, 16=2⁴, 32=2⁵, 9=3², 27=3³, 25=5², 125=5³
Q1 Simplify: 8^(2/3) × 4^(1/2)
Easy
Solution
8^(2/3) = (2³)^(2/3) = 2² = 4
4^(1/2) = (2²)^(1/2) = 2¹ = 2
4 × 2 = 8
Answer 8
🔑 Convert to prime base first. (2³)^(2/3) = 2^(3×2/3) = 2². The fractional exponent shortcut: a^(m/n) = ⁿ√(aᵐ).
Q2 If 2^x = 32, find the value of 2^(x−2).

2^(x−2) = 2^(5−2) = 2³ = 8

Q4 If 4^(x+1) = 64, find x.

Q5 Rationalize the denominator: 1 / (√5 + √2)
Hard
Solution
Multiply top and bottom by conjugate (√5 − √2):
Numerator: (√5 − √2)
Denominator: (√5 + √2)(√5 − √2) = 5 − 2 = 3
Result: (√5 − √2) / 3
Answer (√5 − √2) / 3

33 Logarithms
5 questions▶
📖 How to Attempt — A to Z Notes
Core Idea: log_b(x) = y means b^y = x. It's just a question asking "to what power must I raise b to get x?" That's all it is. Once you internalize that, every log law becomes intuitive.

log_b(xy) = log_b(x) + log_b(y) [Product rule]
log_b(x/y) = log_b(x) − log_b(y) [Quotient rule]
log_b(xⁿ) = n × log_b(x) [Power rule]
log_b(b) = 1 (any base to itself = 1)
log_b(1) = 0 (anything to the power 0 = 1)
Change of base: log_b(x) = log(x) / log(b)
log₁₀(2) ≈ 0.301 | log₁₀(3) ≈ 0.477 | log₁₀(5) ≈ 0.699
ln(e) = 1 | log₁₀(10) = 1 | log₁₀(100) = 2
Approach:
①Convert log expression to exponential form (log_b(x)=y → b^y=x) to verify
②Apply product/quotient/power rules to combine or split logs
③When bases differ: use change of base formula
Q1 Find: log₂(64)
Easy
Solution

log₂(64) = 6
Answer 6
🔑 Read it as: "Power of 2 that gives 64." 2¹=2, 2²=4, 2³=8, 2⁴=16, 2⁵=32, 2⁶=64.
Q2 Simplify: log(12) + log(5) − log(6)
Easy
Solution
= log(12 × 5 / 6) [product rule + quotient rule]
= log(60/6)
= log(10) = 1
Answer 1
🔑 Combine logs first using rules, then evaluate. Addition of logs = log of product. Subtraction = log of quotient.
Q3 If log(x) = 3log(2) + 2log(3), find x.
Medium
Solution

2log(3) = log(3²) = log(9) [power rule]

x = 72
Answer x = 72
🔑 Coefficient in front of log → becomes exponent inside log. Then merge using product rule. Final step: if log(x) = log(k), then x = k.
Q4 If log₁₀(2) = 0.301, find log₁₀(50).

Q5 Solve: log₃(x) + log₃(x − 2) = 1
Hard
Solution
log₃(x(x−2)) = 1 → x(x−2) = 3¹ = 3
x² − 2x − 3 = 0 → (x−3)(x+1) = 0
x = 3 or x = −1. Since log of a negative is undefined, x = 3
Answer x = 3
